Wrestling with the Mystery of Existence

Every now and then, I catch myself trying to wrap my head around how anything exists at all. You know — casual Tuesday thoughts. So here’s a mind-bender: Try to picture "nothing". Not empty space, not darkness — just “nothing.” Tough, right? Almost impossible. That’s because, even so, the notion of “nothing” incorporates the notion of “something.” If not, what remains when you strip everything else away? Space. Time. Matter. Energy. What’s left? Nothing?
It just seems so counterintuitive to me that "something" could come from "absolutely nothing". As stupidly simple as it sounds. All we know from the largest galaxies to the smallest subatomic particles comes from something else. The cosmos or the void of it that we think of as "empty" — is not actually empty. It’s full of energy, buzzing along unseen but palpably there.
Then there’s the Big Bang — the seminal moment when space, time and matter as we know it emerged. But the question that remains, the one that keeps me up at night, is: what happened before? Well, Physics has its ongoing theories, but there is a frontier beyond which explanation evaporates into uncertainty. If “nothing” truly existed before the Big Bang, where did that nothing come from?

Even when we attempt to imagine it, we summon images — blackness, a void — but even that is still "something". It is not the lack of everything; it is the lack of things. And that’s not the same thing as “nothing,” is it?
This complicating factor is made worse by quantum mechanics. It implies that real “nothingness” doesn’t exist — not in the way we instinctively think of it. Even in perfect vacuum, quantum fluctuations — the spontaneous variation in energy — make particles come in and out of existence, briefly. These variations depend on underlying fields, the hidden potential for energy and matter. So, even on the quantum level, “nothing” is really more of a canvas of possibilities than an empty void.
So there must be something larger going on here. Perhaps we will never quite understand it because we are simply not meant to. But this whole universe had to begin with something. Not something as we know it — something beyond our comprehension. A force. A potential. Energy waiting to unfold. Some sort of greater intelligence behind it all, whether we can comprehend it or not.
And that’s where the intrigue comes in. Perhaps we’ll never actually understand all that led to this point. But one thing is certain: whatever started everything, it had to be something. Something with purpose and power. Something that can cause all of this to happen. It’s so hard to think differently, it’s like trying to imagine a totally empty room. The more you envision it, the clearer it becomes that even an empty room isn’t really empty. It’s full of infinite potential, and yet it’s still a room.
It’s a humbling thought. It doesn’t matter whether the universe originated from some random chain of events, or was sculpted by a larger force; regardless, the facts are real and they are undeniable. And here we are — tiny, but inquisitive — trying to figure out where we fit in.
Interesting how one question leads to thousands, huh? But maybe that’s the point. The universe doesn’t show us everything, but it does keep us asking.

The Quiet Audacity of Existence

So how did we get here? What had to happen for you to be reading these words? It's a thought that spirals quickly, unraveling into the deeper mystery of existence itself. After all, the fact that any of this is even happening is so improbable, so delicate, that it almost feels that it has to be intentional. But here we are. And yet, in the grand scheme of the universe, we often forget how remarkable it is just to be. To exist at all. It’s easy to overlook the quiet audacity of our own awareness — until, in a rare moment, you realize the universe didn’t need you to exist. And yet, here you are, marveling at the fact that you do. This isn’t one of those panic-driven existential spirals, don’t worry. I’m not about to dramatically announce I’m moving to the mountains to find meaning in the echoes. No, this is more like standing in line at the grocery store and suddenly realizing I am a person standing in line at the grocery store. A very normal thought that somehow unravels into the absolute absurdity of self-awareness. We show up, somewhere between molecular accidents and deterministic laws, and proceed to act like it’s perfectly normal. But the deeper you think about it, the more it feels like standing in a room without remembering how you got there.

Think about it: here we are—bundles of atoms, stitched together by laws we didn’t write, casually experiencing life as if it’s the most natural thing in the world. Which it is, apparently. But the fact that everything aligns just right for you to sit here reading this? That feels personal. Like the cosmos nudged you and said, “You. You’re up next.” The universe operates with such mathematical precision, it feels as though it was made to fit together perfectly, as if it had a purpose from the start. Constants, forces, and laws calibrate reality so tightly that even minor deviations would dissolve everything we recognize. and yet, here we are, comfortably living within this narrow margin of stability, unaware of the countless conditions quietly conspiring to keep us upright. Take gravity. Shift it slightly—just a fraction of a percent—and stars don’t form. No stars, no planets. No planets, no late-night thoughts about why your charger mysteriously works at specific angles. This isn’t dramatization; it’s physics. the natural order set in motion. The values that shape our universe aren’t mere suggestions—they are essential principles, part of a greater design. Stephen Hawking once pointed out that if the rate of expansion after the Big Bang had been smaller by even one part in a hundred thousand million million, the universe would have collapsed before it even got started. That’s not chance. That’s a configuration So delicate, so improbable, that it has to feel as if a guiding hand is behind it. The question that bugs me the most isn’t why we exist. That’s the kind of puzzle philosophers will keep chewing on until the end of time, (Besides, I think it's a question that can’t really be answered by anything other than faith, which is more about feeling than reasoning. Faith doesn’t need to be logical—that's its essence. But that’s a conversation for another time.). but what gets me is how easily we forget we exist at all. You’d think something as dramatic as being alive would make more noise. But no. Existence sneaks in quietly—like that one friend who’s always at your house but never really announces their arrival. Suddenly, they’re there, eating chips in your kitchen, and you’re wondering when exactly they walked in. Existence operates in the same way. One moment, you’re a kid who thinks the universe stops at the end of your street. The next, you’re a full-grown adult questioning your own existence. And in between, the entire fabric of reality carried on uninterrupted, without ever asking your permission. The philosopher Baruch Spinoza argued that everything exists by necessity—that the universe unfolds in the only way it can. In his view, questioning why we exist is irrelevant because existence is simply the default state of things. So if that’s the case, then the uniqueness isn’t in existing—it’s in being aware that we do. Self-awareness introduces an entirely different layer to the conversation. Consciousness allows the universe to reflect on itself, to examine its own processes from within. Carl Sagan famously said, “We are a way for the cosmos to know itself.” The atoms that once resided in stars now think, observe, and ask questions. Science echoes this sentiment in thermodynamics. Existence, it turns out, is a constant defiance of entropy—a slow decay into disorder. Systems, including living ones, require energy to hold structure against chaos. Your cells sustain organization by exporting entropy elsewhere, buying time against inevitable breakdown. That’s not poetry. it’s a principle of physics. Life isn’t a static state—it is a continuous process sustained by the energy that flows through it, preventing the natural decay toward disorder. While this process appears fragile and temporary, it is upheld by the laws set in place, guiding everything with precision And yet, despite how thin the line is between existence and non-existence, we rarely feel fragile. There’s an odd confidence in the mundane. We trust chairs to hold us without testing their structural integrity. We walk through doorways without questioning if space-time remains continuous. This implicit trust in the mechanics of reality seems trivial, but it reveals something crucial: we assume stability because the universe, by some complex choreography, continues to provide it. But existence doesn’t have to be loud to matter. In fact, it’s the quiet parts—the small moments when nothing big happens—that define it best. There’s a strange comfort in realizing the universe is stable. It doesn’t rearrange itself because you had a bad day. And honestly? That’s reassuring. It means we get to assign meaning. We get to decide if a walk down the street or the laugh of a friend carries weight. I guess that’s the part of existence I keep coming back to—the audacity of it all. the way that atoms form into cells, cells into thoughts, and somehow, in all of that, you emerge. Not just as a body, but as an awareness that occasionally questions the very ground you walk on. The point of this reflection isn't about solving the mystery—it’s about learning to live alongside it. that's, to me, is the most philosophical aspect of existence grounded in science. It’s not just that we exist, but that we are capable of noticing it.

What If My Doubts Are Right After All?

Have you ever stopped to wonder: What if some of the people in my life aren’t real? What if certain things I do or remember only exist in my mind? It’s a fear that can creep in late at night, whispering unsettling possibilities about the fragility of our reality. What if you’re living through experiences that no one else can see or feel? What if the friend you talk to every day, the stranger who smiled at you yesterday, or the argument you replay in your head... never happened? What if they’re figments of your mind, as vivid and convincing as the rest of the world around you? The thought is terrifying. How can you be sure? I know this fear too well; it’s fueled by my anxiety—the same anxiety that makes me question everything. It’s a familiar loop, the constant doubting of what’s real, what’s true, and what I can trust. Most of the time, I manage to push it aside, reminding myself it’s just my overactive mind at work. But then there are moments when I can’t help but wonder: What if my doubt is right? John Nash, a mathematician whose brilliance reshaped modern economics, lived with schizophrenia. His mind created people and scenarios so vivid that he didn’t realize they weren’t real. He trusted them, interacted with them, and built parts of his life around them—only to discover that they existed solely in his mind.

But schizophrenia doesn’t choose only the brilliant or the creative. It’s not confined to geniuses or extraordinary minds. It’s a condition that can touch anyone, regardless of intellect or background. Its terror isn’t tied to intelligence but to the sheer impossibility of knowing what’s real and what isn’t. It’s terrifying to think about. To imagine living years, maybe decades, only to realize that parts of your world were never real. for me sometimes it feels like more than a “what if” that comes and goes. I doubt everything—my choices, my memories, even the people around me. I tell myself this is normal, that it’s just anxiety whispering its usual lies. And yet, the question remains: What if my doubt isn’t wrong after all? It’s an unsettling thought, one that makes me feel as though I’m walking on a tightrope over a chasm of uncertainty. I think of how easily the mind can trick itself, how even someone I know personally has lived with this constant, unrelenting deception. If they couldn’t distinguish between reality and illusion, how can I be so sure of my own world? The truth is, none of us can be absolutely certain about the reality we perceive. Our minds are imperfect, prone to distortions and tricks, especially under stress or anxiety. But maybe that’s not the point. Maybe the point isn’t to know for certain, but to live as authentically as we can within the reality we experience. Even John Nash, once he recognized his illusions, didn’t fight to eliminate them completely. Instead, he learned to live alongside them, focusing on what truly mattered: his work, his family, and his purpose. So, when this fear grips me—when I start to question whether my doubts might be right after all—I try to remind myself of that. It’s okay not to have all the answers. It’s okay to feel uncertain. What matters is how I choose to navigate the reality I have, doubts and all.

Is Reality Fundamentally Mathematical?

Okay, hear me out—what if the universe is basically one giant math problem? Equations everywhere. I mean, everything from the orbit of planets to the way coffee swirls in my mug follows mathematical patterns. Does that mean reality is math? Or are we just obsessed with finding patterns and slapping equations on everything?
The universe isn’t just well-organized—it’s too well-organized. You don’t just get fundamental constants tuned to mind-boggling precision by accident. That’s like saying a fully assembled rocket just fell into place because some nuts and bolts happened to be lying around. Come on! Consider the fine-structure constant (\(\alpha\)), roughly 1/137, which dictates how electrons interact with photons. If it were even slightly different, atoms wouldn’t form as they do, and reality as we know it wouldn’t exist. Coincidence? That’s like rolling a die and getting the same number a trillion times in a row—except worse, because at least with dice, you have a tiny chance.
The Fibonacci sequence pops up in nature like it’s leaving us breadcrumbs. The fine-tuned laws of physics are so precise that it makes you wonder:

Did someone tweak the settings before we got here? It’s almost like reality follows a blueprint—a deliberate design rather than a happy mathematical accident.
The deeper you go, the more everything reduces to numbers. Music, motion, even beauty—it can all be described mathematically. The laws of physics read like an instruction manual written in equations. Take Einstein’s field equations:
\[ G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8 \pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu} \]
That’s the backbone of general relativity, describing how matter bends spacetime. But does that mean math creates reality, or just describes it? It’s like asking if blueprints create a building or just help you understand how it’s made. But let’s not get carried away. If the universe is purely mathematical, why is it also so unpredictable? Quantum mechanics practically laughs at our attempts to make sense of it. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle tells us: \[ \Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2} \] No matter how hard we try, we can’t measure a particle’s position and momentum precisely at the same time. It’s almost as if the universe enjoys keeping secrets.
Maybe math is just the scaffolding, but something greater holds it all together. And let’s be honest—math is only as good as our understanding of it. Maybe the universe *is* mathematically perfect, and we just haven’t cracked the full equation yet. Or maybe our obsession with math is like trying to describe a painting using only numbers—sure, you can assign values to color, texture, and shape, but you’ll never capture the full picture.
Quantum mechanics is one of those subjects that makes perfect sense until you actually think about it. Particles don’t seem to settle on a definite state until they’re observed, which is unsettling when you realize that, on a fundamental level, reality might be playing peekaboo with us. If no one’s looking, does the universe just keep running in the background like an idle process? Or is observation a necessary ingredient for existence? Some physicists, like John Wheeler, think we help shape reality just by observing it. That’s a lot of responsibility. If observation collapses quantum possibilities, does that mean an ultimate Observer has always been there, holding it all together? It’s a humbling thought—that the cosmos is not just a cold, indifferent expanse but something sustained by an ever-present awareness beyond our own. Consider the wavefunction collapse in quantum mechanics: \[ \Psi(x,t) = Ae^{i(kx - \omega t)} \] A particle exists in multiple states at once (superposition) until an observation forces it into one outcome. That’s not just philosophy—it’s tested science. If consciousness is somehow fundamental, does that mean the universe has always been "aware" in some way? Of course, galaxies formed long before we were around to look at them (unless space has a really impressive autopilot mode). The laws of physics don’t seem to care whether we’re paying attention or not. But still, it’s fun to wonder: if reality doesn’t need us to observe it, does it need someone? And if so, who—or what—is watching?
Or maybe perception isn’t about shaping reality but just accessing different layers of it. What if reality is like a vast, multi-dimensional structure, and we only see the slice our senses are tuned to? Just because we don’t perceive something doesn’t mean it isn’t there. We can’t see infrared light or hear dog whistles, but they exist. Maybe the universe has unseen dimensions of existence beyond our limited perspective—things that have been there all along, waiting to be discovered. The Simulation Hypothesis suggests that we’re basically in some advanced civilization’s version of The Sims. If you’ve ever had one of those days where everything feels weirdly scripted, you’ve probably considered this possibility. The idea is that if a civilization could create perfect simulations, then statistically, we’re more likely to be in one than in base reality. But here’s the thing: if we are in a simulation, that still means someone wrote the code. Whether you call that a Creator, a Designer, or something else, it doesn’t exactly remove the idea of intelligent design. Either way, I’d love to see the patch notes for this reality update—preferably with fewer existential bugs. And if this isn’t a simulation, then what’s the alternative? That we exist in a reality so intricate and structured that it could be simulated? Either way, we’re looking at a universe built on astonishing precision. Maintaining a flawless simulation takes a ton of processing power. If this were all a simulation, we might expect to see glitches—like physics breaking down at tiny scales. But so far, the laws of physics hold steady.
If we aren’t in a simulation, though, we’re left with an equally mind-bending question: Why does everything feel so structured, so intentional, so…designed? No matter which way you slice it, the universe isn’t random. It’s too well put together, too fine-tuned, too full of meaning to be a cosmic accident. Maybe reality isn’t something we just accept—maybe it’s something we’re supposed to seek. And maybe, just maybe, at the heart of all this speculation, we’ll find an answer that’s been waiting for us all along.
